Embedded EthicCS @ Harvard: bringing ethical reasoning into the computer science curriculum, because ethics and social responsibility are part of the computer scientist’s work.

Ethical reasoning

Ethical reasoning is an essential skill for today’s computer scientists. Embedded EthiCS teaches students how to think through the ethical and social implications of their work. Our distributive pedagogy model embeds philosophers directly into courses across the standard computer science curriculum, where they develop and teach a module on an ethical issue raised by the course material.

The Embedded EthiCS Mission

Novel computing technologies often improve our lives, but they can also affect them in ways that are harmful or unjust. Facial recognition programs may speed up security lines, but also discriminate against certain populations. Autonomous vehicles may free us from human driver error, but also pose novel risks to human drivers and pedestrians. Today’s computer scientists must learn to design systems that are morally and socially responsible. Embedded EthiCS, a collaborative undertaking of the computer science and philosophy faculties, teaches students of computer science how to think through the ethical and social implications of their work.

The Embedded EthiCS Strategy

Embedded EthiCS meets the challenge of making ethical reasoning integral to computer science education with a distributed pedagogy that embeds philosophers directly into standard computer science courses across the curriculum. The philosopher develops and teaches a module for the course that explores an ethical issue that the course material raises. In a data systems class, the philosopher might explore issues of privacy in large, distributed systems. In a programming language course, she might ask students to consider ethical specifications as well as functional ones. In a human-computer interaction course, she might explore whether software engineers should design systems that are accessible to visually impaired users. In a machine learning class, she might explore how solving problems using machine learning can lead to inadvertent discrimination. In a networks class, she might explore the issue of censorship on social media platforms.

The Embedded EthiCS Advantage

Our distributed pedagogy approach to teaching ethics in computer science has three advantages over stand-alone computer ethics courses. First, the approach shows students the extent to which ethical issues permeate virtually all areas of computer science. Second, it familiarizes students with the wide range of ethical issues arising across the field. And third, it provides students with repeated practice reasoning through those issues and communicating about those positions. Embedded EthiCS thus habituates students to think of ethical and social concerns as central to their work as computer scientists, rather than as specialized topics best reserved for experts.

The Embedded EthiCS Skill Set

There is, of course, no simple recipe for resolving ethical problems in computer science (or any other field). Rather than advocate a one-size-fits-all approach to ethical problems, the Embedded EthiCS teaches students to:
  1. identify and anticipate moral issues in their work;
  2. think clearly about those moral issues, both alone and collaboratively; and
  3. communicate their understanding of those moral issues effectively.
In short, the goal of Embedded EthiCS is to teach students to consider not merely what technologies they could create, but what technologies they should create.
